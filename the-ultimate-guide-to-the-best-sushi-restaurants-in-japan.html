The Ultimate Guide to the Best Sushi Restaurants in Japan

By jesse martin
April 17, 2025

 

Japan is the birthplace of sushi, a culinary art form that has captivated food lovers worldwide. From the bustling streets of Tokyo to the serene coastal towns of Hokkaido, Japan boasts an unparalleled variety of sushi restaurants, ranging from humble conveyor-belt joints (*kaiten-zushi*) to exclusive, Michelin-starred *omakase* experiences where master chefs craft each piece with precision. Whether you're seeking the freshest *otoro* (fatty tuna), the perfect *nigiri*, or innovative modern twists on traditional sushi, Japan offers an unforgettable gastronomic journey. Below, we explore some of the best sushi restaurants across the country, each offering a unique experience that reflects Japan’s deep culinary heritage.

1. Sukiyabashi Jiro (Tokyo) – The Legendary Omakase Experience
No list of Japan’s best sushi restaurants would be complete without mentioning **Sukiyabashi Jiro**, the three-Michelin-starred establishment immortalized in the documentary *Jiro Dreams of Sushi*. Located in Ginza, Tokyo, this tiny 10-seat counter is helmed by **Jiro Ono**, a living legend in the sushi world. At 98 years old, Jiro has spent over seven decades perfecting his craft, and his dedication is evident in every bite.

The restaurant offers a no-frills **20-piece omakase** (chef’s selection) that changes daily based on the freshest ingredients available at Tsukiji Market (now Toyosu Market). Highlights include:
- Meltingly tender *otoro* (fatty tuna)
- Perfectly balanced *akagai* (ark clam)
- Uni (sea urchin) so creamy it dissolves on the tongue

Dining here is a once-in-a-lifetime experience, though securing a reservation is notoriously difficult (bookings must be made months in advance through a high-end hotel concierge). While some debate whether it’s still the "best" sushi in Japan, its historical significance and Jiro’s relentless pursuit of perfection make it a must-visit for serious sushi connoisseurs.

2. Sushi Saito (Tokyo) – The New King of Sushi
If Sukiyabashi Jiro represents the old guard, **Sushi Saito** is the modern benchmark for sushi excellence. With three Michelin stars and a reputation as one of the hardest restaurants in the world to book (only 7 seats, often reserved years in advance), chef **Takashi Saito** has earned global acclaim for his flawless technique and sublime flavors.

Located in the upscale Ark Hills district, Saito’s sushi is characterized by:
- **Shari (sushi rice) seasoned with a perfect red vinegar blend**
- **Fish aged to peak tenderness**
- **An omakase experience that feels intimate and effortless**

Standout pieces include the **kohada (gizzard shad)**, which is marinated to achieve a delicate balance of sweetness and acidity, and the **anago (sea eel)**, brushed with a secret sauce that enhances its natural richness. Unlike Jiro’s strict, fast-paced service, Saito offers a more relaxed atmosphere, allowing diners to savor each piece at their own pace.

3. Kyubey (Tokyo) – A Timeless Ginza Institution
For those who can’t secure a seat at Jiro or Saito, **Kyubey** in Ginza is an excellent alternative—equally prestigious but slightly more accessible. Founded in 1935, this legendary sushi-ya has trained many of Japan’s top chefs and remains a favorite among locals and tourists alike.

Kyubey’s signature style includes:
- Warm, slightly loose shari that complements the fish perfectly
- Generous portions of uni and ikura (salmon roe)
- A slightly sweet tamagoyaki (egg omelet) that melts in the mouth

The restaurant offers both counter seating and private rooms, making it ideal for groups. Unlike ultra-exclusive omakase spots, Kyubey allows à la carte ordering, so diners can focus on their favorite pieces.

4. Sawada (Tokyo) – An Intimate, Artistic Experience
With only six seats, **Sawada** is one of Tokyo’s most exclusive sushi bars. Chef **Koji Sawada**, a former fisherman, brings an unparalleled understanding of seafood to his craft, selecting only the finest seasonal ingredients.

Highlights of Sawada’s omakase include:
- Kuruma ebi (Japanese tiger prawn) served at the perfect temperature
- Aged katsuo (bonito) with a smoky, deep flavor
- Hand-grated wasabi for a fresh, spicy kick

The experience is intimate, with Sawada himself engaging guests in conversation while crafting each piece with surgical precision. Reservations are extremely difficult to secure, often requiring a Japanese phone number or a trusted referral.

5. Yoshitake (Tokyo) – A Michelin-Starred Hidden Gem
Tucked away in a Ginza backstreet, **Sushi Yoshitake** is a three-Michelin-starred gem led by chef **Masahiro Yoshitake**, known for his innovative approach to traditional Edomae sushi.

What sets Yoshitake apart:
- A focus on aged fish for deeper umami
- Unique toppings like smoked skipjack tuna
- A delicate balance of flavors in every bite

The omakase here is a masterclass in subtlety, with each piece designed to highlight the fish’s natural taste without overpowering it.

6. Harutaka (Tokyo) – Jiro’s Protégé Shines
Trained under Jiro Ono, **Harutaka Takahashi** now runs his own acclaimed sushi bar in Ginza. While influenced by his mentor, Harutaka’s style is slightly more modern, with a focus on:
- Exceptional tuna cuts, including rare chutoro
- Bright, vinegared rice that enhances the fish
- A welcoming atmosphere compared to Jiro’s strictness

7. Sushi Mizutani (Tokyo) – Another Jiro Alumni Masterpiece
Before its closure in 2016, **Sushi Mizutani** was one of Tokyo’s most revered sushi spots. While the original is gone, former apprentices have opened their own restaurants, keeping Mizutani’s legacy alive with the same meticulous standards.

8. Sushi Dai & Daiwa Sushi (Tokyo) – Best for Toyosu Market Visitors
Located in Toyosu Market (the successor to Tsukiji), these two legendary spots are famous for their ultra-fresh, market-price sushi. Expect long lines, but the melt-in-your-mouth **otoro** and **uni** are worth the wait.

9. Sushi Iwa (Kyoto) – Refined Kansai-Style Sushi
Kyoto’s **Sushi Iwa** offers a more delicate, refined style compared to Tokyo’s bold flavors. Chef **Hisayoshi Iwa** focuses on:
- Elegant presentations
- Subtle, balanced flavors
- Seasonal Kyoto ingredients

10. Sushi Yoshitomi (Fukuoka) – Kyushu’s Finest
In Fukuoka, **Sushi Yoshitomi** stands out for its creative omakase, incorporating local Kyushu seafood like **mentaiko (spicy cod roe)** and **buri (yellowtail)**.


Final Thoughts
Japan’s sushi scene is vast and varied, with each restaurant offering a unique interpretation of this ancient craft. Whether you seek the austere perfection of Jiro, the warm hospitality of Kyubey, or the innovative flair of Yoshitake, one thing is certain: sushi in Japan is an art form, and every bite tells a story.

For the ultimate experience, book well in advance, arrive with an open mind, and savor each piece like it’s your last—because in Japan, sushi isn’t just food; it’s a way of life.
