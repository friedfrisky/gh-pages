A Guide to Japan's Unique Festivals Throughout the Year

By jesse martin
April 22, 2025


Japan is a country rich in culture, tradition, and vibrant celebrations. From ancient Shinto rituals to lively modern events, Japan’s festivals—known as matsuri—offer unforgettable experiences. Each festival reflects regional history, seasonal changes, and local customs. Whether you’re drawn to fiery spectacles, delicate cherry blossoms, or thrilling parades, Japan’s festivals provide year-round excitement. This guide explores the most unique and captivating festivals across Japan, ensuring you don’t miss a single highlight.  

 January: Hatsumode and Sapporo Snow Festival  

January in Japan begins with Hatsumode, the first shrine visit of the year. Millions flock to temples like Meiji Shrine in Tokyo or Fushimi Inari in Kyoto to pray for good fortune. Later in the month, the Sapporo Snow Festival transforms Hokkaido into a winter wonderland. Massive ice sculptures, illuminated at night, attract over two million visitors. This festival is a must-see for snow lovers and photographers alike.  

February: Setsubun and Yokote Kamakura Festival  

February kicks off with Setsubun, a lively bean-throwing ceremony to ward off evil spirits. Families chant Oni wa soto! Fuku wa uchi!" ("Demons out! Luck in!") while tossing roasted soybeans. Meanwhile, the Yokote Kamakura Festival in Akita features adorable snow igloos lit by candles. Visitors enjoy amazake (sweet sake) and mochi (rice cakes) inside these cozy domes. This intimate winter festival offers a magical, fairytale-like atmosphere.  


March: Omizutori and Hirosaki Cherry Blossom Festival  

March marks the beginning of spring with Omizutori at Nara’s Todai-ji Temple. This 1,200-year-old Buddhist ritual involves giant flaming torches showering sparks for purification. Meanwhile, the Hirosaki Cherry Blossom Festival in Aomori dazzles with over 2,500 sakura trees. The castle grounds turn pink, and night illuminations create a dreamy ambiance. It’s one of Japan’s top spots for hanami (flower viewing).  


April: Takayama Matsuri and Kanamara Penis Festival  

April’s Takayama Matsuri in Gifu showcases ornate floats and intricate karakuri puppetry. This UNESCO-listed event is a feast for the eyes with its Edo-period craftsmanship. In contrast, Tokyo’s Kanamara Penis Festival is a wild, risqué celebration of fertility. Giant phallus-shaped shrines parade through the streets, drawing laughter and cheers. Both festivals highlight Japan’s blend of elegance and eccentricity.  

 

May: Kanda Matsuri and Aoi Matsuri  

May brings Kanda Matsuri, one of Tokyo’s grandest Shinto festivals. Portable shrines (mikoshi) are carried through the streets amid drumming and chanting. Meanwhile, Kyoto’s Aoi Matsuri features a stunning procession in Heian-era costumes. Over 500 participants march from the Imperial Palace to Shimogamo Shrine. These festivals offer deep dives into Japan’s spiritual and historical roots.  

June: Sanno Matsuri and Chagu Chagu Umakko  

June’s Sanno Matsuri in Tokyo is another major Shinto event, with mikoshi parades and sacred rituals. The Chagu Chagu Umakko in Iwate is a charming horse parade where ponies wear colorful bells and decorations. This festival honors horses’ role in agriculture and is especially popular with families. Both events provide unique cultural insights.  

July: Gion Matsuri and Tenjin Matsuri  

July is festival peak season, starting with Kyoto’s Gion Matsuri, a month-long celebration with massive floats. Osaka’s Tenjin Matsuri lights up the river with fireworks and boat processions. These festivals blend ancient traditions with summer energy. The vibrant yukata (summer kimono) crowds add to the festive spirit.  


August: Awa Odori and Nebuta Matsuri  

August’s Awa Odori in Tokushima is Japan’s biggest dance festival, with thousands chanting "Fools dance, fools watch!" Meanwhile, Aomori’s Nebuta Matsuri features gigantic illuminated floats of warriors and gods. Both events are high-energy, making them summer highlights.  


September: Kishiwada Danjiri Matsuri  

September’s Kishiwada Danjiri Matsuri is a thrilling (and dangerous) cart-pulling race in Osaka. Teams dash through streets with heavy wooden floats, risking collisions. This festival is adrenaline-packed and deeply rooted in community pride.  


October: Kurama no Hi Matsuri  

October’s Kurama no Hi Matsuri near Kyoto involves massive torches carried through a tiny mountain village. The fiery procession honors a deity who protects against disasters. It’s a mystical, lesser-known gem.  


November: Shichi-Go-San and Tori-no-Ichi  

November’s Shichi-Go-San celebrates children aged 3, 5, and 7 with shrine visits and lucky candy. Meanwhile, Tokyo’s Tori-no-Ichi fair sells bamboo rakes for good luck in business. Both festivals are deeply symbolic.  


December: Chichibu Night Festival  

December ends with the Chichibu Night Festival, featuring dazzling floats and fireworks. This winter spectacle is perfect for a festive finale to the year.  

Final Thoughts  

Japan’s festivals offer year-round cultural immersion. Whether you seek tradition, excitement, or beauty, there’s a matsuri for every traveler. Plan your trip around these unforgettable events!
