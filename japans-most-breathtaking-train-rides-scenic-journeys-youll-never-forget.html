Japan’s Most Breathtaking Train Rides: Scenic Journeys You’ll Never Forget

By jesse martin
April 25, 2025


There’s something magical about train travel—the rhythmic clatter of wheels on tracks, the ever-changing scenery outside your window, and the thrill of adventure as you glide through landscapes you’d never see from a highway. Nowhere is this more true than in Japan, a country where trains aren’t just a way to get from point A to point B, but an experience in themselves.  

Forget the bullet train for a moment (though it’s a marvel in its own right). Beyond the speed of the Shinkansen lies a world of slow, scenic railways that take you through misty valleys, along rugged coastlines, and past rolling fields of lavender so vivid they look like something out of a dream. These trains aren’t just transportation—they’re moving observation decks, offering front-row seats to Japan’s most stunning natural wonders.  


               The Sagano Romantic Train: Kyoto’s Open-Air Masterpiece 

Picture this: You’re sitting in an old-fashioned, open-air train car, the wind gently brushing your face as you glide alongside the Hozugawa River. Towering bamboo groves sway in the breeze, their emerald-green stalks filtering the sunlight into a soft, dappled glow. In spring, cherry blossoms drift lazily onto the tracks; in autumn, the surrounding mountains explode in fiery shades of red and gold.  

This is the Sagano Romantic Train, one of Japan’s most beloved scenic railways. Running between Saga Torokko Station and Kameoka, this charming 25-minute journey feels like stepping into a Studio Ghibli film. The train moves slowly, deliberately, as if it knows you’ll want to savor every second. And you will—especially when you pass through the heart of Arashiyama’s famous bamboo forest, where sunlight filters through the dense stalks in a way that feels almost otherworldly.  

Pro tip: Book the last train of the day in autumn. As dusk falls, the train slows to a crawl through the most scenic stretches, and the surrounding foliage is illuminated by soft lantern light. It’s pure magic.  


                    The Tadami Line: Japan’s Most Photographed Train Ride

If you’ve ever seen a postcard-perfect shot of a train crossing a red bridge over a misty river, framed by snow-capped mountains, chances are it was taken along the JR Tadami Line. This remote route in Fukushima and Niigata prefectures is a hidden gem, winding through villages so small they don’t even have convenience stores, past rice paddies that glow golden in the summer sun, and alongside the Tadami River, whose crystal-clear waters mirror the surrounding forests.  

The most iconic view? Tadami River Bridge No. 1, where the train curves gracefully over the water, perfectly framed by the valley below. Visit in winter, and you’ll see the entire scene dusted in snow, the train’s warm interior lights contrasting against the icy landscape outside. It’s no wonder photographers from around the world flock here—this is Japan’s countryside at its most untouched and serene.  

 


                               The Seven Stars in Kyushu: Luxury on Rails  

What if your train ride came with a private butler, gourmet kaiseki meals, and a bedroom with a view of active volcanoes? Enter the Seven Stars in Kyushu, Japan’s most luxurious sleeper train—a rolling five-star hotel that takes you through the wild, varied landscapes of Kyushu.  

This isn’t just a train; it’s an experience. Each cabin is crafted from rare woods and adorned with hand-painted motifs inspired by Kyushu’s nature. The dining car serves dishes made with local ingredients, from Kagoshima’s famed Kurobuta pork to fresh seafood caught that morning. And the route? Spectacular. You’ll pass steaming hot springs, rugged coastlines, and even Sakurajima, one of Japan’s most active volcanoes, its peak often wreathed in smoke.  

The highlight? Waking up to the sight of Yufuin, a picturesque onsen town nestled between forested hills and Mount Yufu’s twin peaks. As you sip matcha in your private suite, watching the mist rise over the countryside, you’ll understand why this journey books out months—sometimes years—in advance.  

 


                    The Izu Kyuko Line: Where the Ocean Meets the Rails  

Most visitors to Japan stick to the well-trodden paths of Tokyo and Kyoto, but those in the know head to the Izu Peninsula, a rugged, coastal paradise just two hours from the capital. And the best way to see it? The Izu Kyuko Line, a winding route that clings to cliffs, dives through tunnels, and offers jaw-dropping views of the Pacific at every turn.  

Ride the Odoriko Limited Express, and you’ll be treated to panoramas of sheer ocean bluffs, hidden beaches, and—if you’re lucky—a glimpse of Mount Fuji in the distance. Stop at Jogasaki Coast, where a suspension bridge stretches over crashing waves, or hop off in Atami, a historic hot spring town where you can soak in an onsen while watching the sunset over the sea.  

                         Why These Train Rides Belong on Your Bucket List  

Japan’s scenic trains do more than just take you places—they make the journey the destination. Whether you’re sipping sake as cherry blossoms flutter past your window, standing on an open-air carriage with the wind in your hair, or waking up to a volcano outside your luxury suite, these rides are unforgettable.  

So next time you plan a trip to Japan, don’t just rush from city to city. Slow down. Take the scenic route. Because sometimes, the best way to see a country isn’t from a plane or a highway—it’s from the window of a train, watching the world go by in all its breathtaking beauty.
